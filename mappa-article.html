MAPPA Studio Faces Backlash
From Workers

Animators are pushed to the limit in the name of entertainment

Elaina Niederer November 2023 Class Assignment — Multimedia Storytelling · Seattle University

Last month, anime enthusiasts were buzzing about MAPPA Studios' working conditions as animators voiced concerns over grueling work hours and challenging conditions. Renowned for biting off more than it can chew, the studio faced a backlash for placing a heavy burden on its workforce.

The latest episodes of Jujutsu Kaisen, a fan-favorite anime, stirred skepticism and debate over animation quality. While the storyline remained captivating, inconsistencies in the animation prompted fans to look closer — and what they found alarmed them. Rumors of non-disclosure agreements surfaced, preventing animators from openly discussing their experiences and further fueling concerns about the toll production was taking on their well-being.

Reports suggested that animators were subjected to intense work schedules — six to seven days a week, around 12 hours each day. On September 30, speculation arose that the animators of Jujutsu Kaisen were under a non-disclosure agreement, hindering them from expressing dissatisfaction with the production.

Animators Speak Out

On November 14, 2023, a wave of animators, animation directors, and key animators took to Twitter to shed light on the troubling conditions during production of Jujutsu Kaisen Season 2. The notable figures expressing their concerns included Itsuki Tsuchigami (episode 16 director), Kazuto Arai (episode 13 director and key animator in episode 16), Shunsuke Okubo (episode 12 director), and prominent animator Hakuyu Go.

Tsuchigami, known for his previous critiques of studio practices, conveyed mixed feelings about MAPPA receiving praise for JJK S2. While acknowledging the hard work of in-house staff, he expressed discomfort with individual achievements being credited to the studio. He reposted past tweets highlighting the challenging schedule and poor planning of the production, citing an apparent NDA preventing him from naming the studio directly.

Sota Shigetsugu
Key animator, episodes 14 & 16

"The worst cycle is when the staff members all somehow manage to complete a schedule that would be impossible to meet under normal circumstances, while seeing hell, and the higher-ups who only saw the results misunderstand and neglect to improve, thinking 'It's not that bad, it can be done!' I wonder if the beginning of this was when they completed a movie in four months…"

Shigetsugu's post captured a dynamic that animators described repeatedly: extraordinary effort being used as justification for continued overwork, rather than prompting structural improvement.

In a striking call to action, Hakuyu Go — not yet credited as an animator in JJK S2 at the time — urged people to quit MAPPA and discard their key cards on the way out, shedding light on the severity of conditions animators were enduring. His posts also emphasized that business partners and board members took precedence over the well-being of animators, raising questions about the industry's priorities.

A Pattern Beyond One Show

MAPPA's demanding work environment also became evident in the production of Jujutsu Kaisen 0, a prequel film. Despite its financial success, the film was completed in just four months — compared to the typical two-to-three-year timeframe for comparable productions. Various animators, in posts that were later deleted, leaked allegations about working conditions that were subsequently corroborated by others.

"I have so much love for the show, it feels so wrong to see such beautiful art and an epic story unfold whilst knowing the talented creatives are suffering in order to entertain us."

— Anna Schreiner, fan of Jujutsu Kaisen, found via social research

Criticism extended beyond Jujutsu Kaisen. Animators meeting tight deadlines were allegedly left unsupported by higher-ups, sparking broader debate about how the anime industry treats its creative workforce. The controversy prompted fans to weigh their own enjoyment against the human cost behind the productions they love — a conversation that continues today.

As fans awaited the continuation of Jujutsu Kaisen Season 2, the backlash cast a shadow on the anime community and raised urgent questions: when audiences reward studios with record viewership and revenue, are they also rewarding systems that exploit the people who make it all possible?
