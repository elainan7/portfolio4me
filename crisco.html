Polyunsaturating
the Population

A research paper examining how Crisco used advertising to reshape American perceptions of health, food, and domestic roles in the early 20th century — analyzed through critical discourse analysis and constitutive rhetoric.

Paper Overview

"This paper examines how Crisco used advertising to reshape American perceptions of health, food, and domestic roles in the early 20th century — exploring how its marketing constructed a narrative that framed the product as modern, scientific, and morally superior to traditional animal fats, while targeting women as caretakers responsible for family health."

Research Focus

01

Constitutive Rhetoric & Brand Narrative

Applied the theory of constitutive rhetoric to analyze how Crisco's advertising didn't just sell a product — it constructed a new consumer identity. By positioning Crisco as modern and scientific, the brand fabricated a reality that reshaped how Americans thought about cooking, health, and domestic virtue.

02

Gendered Messaging & Cultural Symbolism

Examined how Crisco's marketing specifically targeted women as caretakers, positioning them as responsible for family health and modern domesticity. Analyzed the cultural symbolism embedded in the brand's use of scientific language, cleanliness imagery, and moral framing to drive adoption over traditional animal fats.

03

Ethical Implications of Strategic Messaging

Assessed the long-term ethical implications of Crisco's campaign — a fabricated yet persuasive health narrative that influenced consumer identity and beliefs about nutrition for decades. Connected historical advertising strategies to contemporary questions about ethical marketing and brand responsibility.

Why This Matters

Marketing shapes more than buying habits

Crisco's early campaigns are a case study in how strategic communication can construct entire belief systems — not just product preferences. The brand didn't just win market share; it rewrote what "healthy" cooking meant in American homes for a generation.

This project pushed me to think critically about the ethical weight of communication work — and about the responsibility that comes with shaping how people understand the world around them.

"Crisco's marketing didn't just sell a product — it constructed a new way of thinking about health, modernity, and the role of women in the kitchen. That's constitutive rhetoric in action."

Skills Demonstrated

Rhetorical Analysis

Deconstructing strategic messaging to understand how language, imagery, and framing construct meaning beyond the literal content.

Brand Storytelling

Analyzing how brands build identity and influence belief — and what it means when that influence operates without transparency.

Ethical Advertising

Thinking critically about the responsibilities of communication professionals — especially when messaging shapes culture, not just behavior.
